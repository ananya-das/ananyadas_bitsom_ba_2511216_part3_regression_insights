# Final Recommendation

## Executive Summary

A multiple regression analysis was conducted to identify the factors most strongly associated with monthly sales performance across stores. The final model included marketing spend, footfall, inventory availability, customer rating, region, and store type variables.

The selected model achieved an **R² of 0.831**, indicating that approximately **83.1% of the variation in monthly sales** is explained by the variables included in the analysis. This suggests that the model provides a strong basis for understanding key sales drivers and supporting business decision-making.

---

# Key Factors Associated with Monthly Sales

The analysis identified the following variables as having the strongest and most statistically significant relationships with monthly sales:

## 1. Footfall

* Coefficient: 34.015
* P-value: 6.25 × 10⁻¹⁰⁵

Footfall was the strongest predictor of monthly sales in the model. Stores with higher customer traffic consistently generated higher sales. This finding highlights the importance of attracting customers into stores through marketing, location strategy, and customer engagement initiatives.

## 2. Inventory Availability Percentage

* Coefficient: 3047.929
* P-value: 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁰

Inventory availability showed a strong positive relationship with sales performance. Stores with better stock availability were more likely to achieve higher sales because customers were able to purchase desired products without encountering stock-outs.

## 3. Marketing Spend

* Coefficient: 1.209
* P-value: 2.44 × 10⁻¹⁹

Marketing spend demonstrated a positive and statistically significant relationship with monthly sales. Increased marketing investment appears to support higher sales by driving awareness and customer visits.

## 4. Customer Rating

* Coefficient: 13,439.363
* P-value: 0.005

Customer rating was positively associated with sales performance. Stores with stronger customer satisfaction ratings generally achieved higher revenue, suggesting that customer experience contributes to business success.

## 5. Store Type

Compared with Residential stores:

* Airport Stores: +42,659 monthly sales
* Mall Stores: +29,382 monthly sales
* High Street Stores: +18,098 monthly sales

All three store formats showed statistically significant positive relationships with monthly sales, with Airport stores demonstrating the strongest performance premium.

# Variables Leadership Should Focus On

Based on the regression results, leadership should prioritize:

### Increasing Customer Footfall

Customer traffic was the strongest sales driver identified in the analysis. Initiatives that attract more visitors into stores are likely to have the greatest impact on revenue growth.

### Improving Inventory Availability

Maintaining high inventory availability can help reduce lost sales opportunities and improve overall store performance.

### Optimizing Marketing Investments

Marketing spend showed a clear positive relationship with sales and should continue to be used strategically to support customer acquisition and store traffic.

### Enhancing Customer Experience

Improving customer satisfaction and service quality may contribute to higher sales through increased loyalty and repeat purchases.

### Prioritizing High-Performing Store Formats

Airport, Mall, and High Street stores demonstrated stronger performance than Residential stores and may warrant greater investment attention when considering expansion opportunities.

# Variables That Should Not Be Over-Interpreted

The following variables were not statistically significant in the final model:

## Region_North

* P-value: 0.084

## Region_South

* P-value: 0.823

After controlling for other variables, there is insufficient evidence that North and South region stores perform differently from West region stores. Leadership should therefore avoid making major strategic decisions based solely on these regional coefficients.

Although Region_East was statistically significant, regional effects should still be interpreted cautiously because local market conditions and store-specific factors may vary considerably within each region.

# Recommended Business Actions

Based on the findings, the following actions are recommended:

1. Increase initiatives that drive store traffic, including targeted marketing campaigns and local customer engagement programs.
2. Maintain high inventory availability to minimize lost sales caused by stock-outs.
3. Continue investing in marketing activities that demonstrate measurable returns.
4. Improve customer experience through staff training and service quality initiatives.
5. Prioritize Airport, Mall, and High Street formats when evaluating future store investments or expansion opportunities.
6. Conduct additional analysis on underperforming East-region locations to identify operational improvement opportunities.

---

# Risks and Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the results:

* The model does not include competitor activity, local demographics, economic conditions, weather, or seasonality.
* Store-level operational factors may influence sales but are not captured in the available dataset.
* Residual analysis identified stores that significantly outperformed or underperformed model predictions, suggesting that additional drivers of sales remain unobserved.
* The analysis is based on historical data and may not fully reflect future market conditions.

---

# Association Does Not Automatically Mean Causation

Regression analysis identifies statistical relationships between variables and sales performance, but it does not automatically prove that one variable causes another.

For example:

* Higher marketing spend is associated with higher sales, but the analysis cannot prove that marketing alone caused the increase.
* Stores with higher footfall generate higher sales, but strong sales may also attract more customers.
* Customer ratings may be associated with higher sales, but both could be influenced by other factors such as store quality or management effectiveness.

Establishing causation typically requires controlled experiments, randomized testing, or additional causal analysis methods.

Therefore, leadership should view these findings as evidence of strong associations that can guide decision-making rather than definitive proof of cause-and-effect relationships.

---

# Final Recommendation

The multiple regression model should be used as the primary decision-support tool because it explains approximately 83.1% of monthly sales variation and identifies several actionable business drivers.

Leadership should focus on increasing customer footfall, maintaining high inventory availability, investing strategically in marketing, and improving customer experience. Airport, Mall, and High Street store formats appear to offer stronger sales potential than Residential stores and should receive greater consideration in future investment decisions.

While the results provide valuable insights into sales performance, strategic decisions should also consider operational knowledge, market conditions, and additional business context beyond the variables included in the regression model.
